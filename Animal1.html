Save Them Before It's Too Late!

An alarming look at why the current extinction crisis demands immediate public awareness and action. Insights from frontline conservationist Bumi Senandika reveal why this is more than a moral issue, it's about human survival.

The extinction rate today is 1000 times higher than in the pre-human era. According to IUCN data in the Red List records, 28% of animal species are threatened with extinction and are in endangered status. More than 1 million plant and animal species are threatened with extinction due to human activities.

Direct from Bumi Senandika: "Climate change and extreme weather pose a serious threat to species habitats, rising temperatures, extreme forest fires initially caused by deforestation. If greenhouse gas emissions continue as they are now, it's certain that one-third of species could become extinct by 2050."
Photographs by Rai (@nayzta)

The Human Footprint: Habitat Loss and Biodiversity Collapse

The loss of animal habitats is largely due to human activity—land conversion into urban areas and agriculture has altered 70% of Earth's ice-free land, which then causes the loss of biodiversity. Ecosystems like coral reefs and the Arctic have already suffered severe damage, affecting species such as polar bears and corals.

"If our biodiversity disappears, it will increase impacts for us as humans—it could threaten essential ecosystem services such as carbon absorption, water filtration, and pollination of plants that support global food security."

Animals also play a role as natural "carbon sinks"—losing them could accelerate natural climate change. Additionally, wildlife trade and intensive farming increase the risk of zoonotic pandemics. As Bumi explains, "Although there has been progress such as agreements at COP16 (2024-2025) for biodiversity funding and bans on biodiversity trade in some countries, implementation is still slow."

Ecosystem Alert: Coral Reefs

Coral reefs, home to 25% of marine life, are experiencing massive bleaching events due to rising sea temperatures. Without immediate global climate action, we risk losing these vital ecosystems entirely within decades, causing cascading extinctions throughout ocean food chains.

Photographs by Rai (@nayzta)

The Connection Between Human Lifestyle Choices and Animal Threats

The relationship between human lifestyle and threats to animals is very close: consumptive lifestyles and human habitat expansion threaten wildlife through habitat loss, resource conflicts, and illegal hunting, while in the realm of pets, irresponsible lifestyles (neglecting basic needs, cruel treatment) create suffering.

The Competition for Resources

Increasing human population and development creates competition for resources (food, water, land) with wildlife, forcing them out of their natural habitats and increasing conflicts (crop damage, livestock predation, disease transmission), which often ends in the eradication of wild animals.

Bumi notes, "Often, humans keep animals only as objects of entertainment, not as living beings that need responsibility. That's why since the beginning of Hewanku Zoo, its main focus has been on conservation rather than providing circus entertainment because that could become dangerous animal exploitation."

Consumer Alert: Wildlife Products

Demand for animal products (skins, medicines) drives illegal hunting and trade, threatening vulnerable species. Every purchase of ivory, exotic skins, or traditional medicines containing animal parts directly funds extinction.

Small but Meaningful Actions: What Individuals Can Do Daily

We can start with the easiest things that we can apply at home in our daily lives by reducing our carbon footprint, saving energy, recycling, and reducing waste to combat climate change that threatens animal habitats.

Daily Choices That Matter

Avoid buying wildlife products such as skins, fur, and ivory to prevent illegal hunting. Provide safe spaces for insects—instead of killing ants, we can sweep them and move them outside to a safer place. Provide drinking water for birds in summer. Reduce pollution that can damage nature—don't throw trash in rivers, reduce the use of harmful chemicals.

We can also participate in volunteer activities, reforestation, beach cleanups, and take part in tree planting programs to improve air quality by producing oxygen and absorbing carbon dioxide, preventing floods and soil erosion which can provide habitat for wildlife.

Simple Action: Responsible Tourism

When going on recreation, be a responsible tourist. Choose ecotourism that supports conservation activities and avoid places that captivate by making wildlife entertainment. Observe wildlife from a distance, don't disturb them, don't feed wild animals in the wild randomly.

The Crucial Role of Media and Storytelling

The media plays a crucial role in shaping narratives and storytelling that can increase awareness and empathy for animal welfare. Media can present it through visuals and emotions, and mobilize public action, so that perception from object to subject can change the narrative from "what animals can provide" to "what animals feel and need."

Education Through Engagement

Media is a platform that we can use as an educational source—by participating in spreading accurate information about threats, needs, and how to care for animals through articles, videos, or campaigns. These can be our small steps to protect them in simple ways.

As Bumi emphasizes, "This will place them as subjects of care, although it can also be a double-edged sword if sensational or ignores root problems. The power of storytelling can transform indifference into action when done responsibly."

Media Impact: Visual Documentation

Photographs like those by Rai (@nayzta) in this article provide visual evidence that moves people beyond statistics. Seeing animals in their habitats—or what remains of them—creates emotional connections that drive lasting change.

A Message to the Younger Generation: Compassion, Coexistence, and Responsibility

In essence, we have extraordinary power to create a world where humans and animals can coexist with full compassion and mutual respect. Let's learn to see animals not as objects, but as fellow living beings who can feel joy, pain, fear—just like us humans.

"Give compassion and our responsibility as beings who can better protect them. Give simple empathy like: feeding wild animals wisely, helping neglected animals, choosing to adopt rather than buy pets."

Coexistence Means Sharing Earth Fairly

Our daily choices—what food we buy—determine whether forests still stand for tigers and orangutans, whether oceans are still safe for whales and turtles, or whether farm animals are treated with respect. Therefore, let's work together to reduce plastic, eat plant-based more often, and support animal-friendly products.

This Responsibility Is Ours

We are the most environmentally conscious generation in history. Let's join hands with one voice, provide campaigns, peaceful protests, create content on social media to stand on the side of animals, defend them, protect them, become their front guard. Be activists in schools, communities, or even at home to educate people around you.

Bumi's closing message: "A good world is not a dream—it's a choice we make in daily life. If I, you, and all of us have compassion, coexistence will follow, and responsibility will become our strength. Animals on this planet are waiting for us to speak up. Please don't wait until it's too late. Start now, because their future is our future."

Leading Indonesia's Race Against Extinction

Bumi Senandika Mahiswara, widely known as Bumi, was born on February 14, 1992, in West Jakarta, DKI Jakarta. Standing at 183 cm, he has built his life and career around a deep commitment to environmental protection and ecological restoration. He completed his undergraduate studies in Environmental Engineering at the Bandung Institute of Technology (2010–2015), followed by a Master's degree in Environmental Engineering at The University of Melbourne (2015–2018), where he further refined his technical and strategic understanding of sustainable environmental management.

Professional Journey

Beginning his professional journey in 2018, Bumi served as a Field Coordinator at Burung Indonesia until 2021, leading the Endemic Java–Bali Bird Habitat Restoration Program, where he worked closely with local communities and conservation practitioners on the ground. From 2021 to 2023, he took on the role of Restoration Lead at Yayasan Konservasi Alam Nusantara (YKAN), The Nature Conservancy Indonesia, overseeing large-scale ecosystem restoration initiatives.

Since 2023, Bumi has been the Co-Founder and Executive Director of Prakriti Nusantara, a non-profit organization integrated with a social enterprise model, dedicated to primary forest reforestation, the private acquisition of critical lands for permanent protection, and the development of wildlife corridors for flagship species, reflecting his long-term vision for sustainable and lasting conservation impact in Indonesia.

Conservation Philosophy

Bumi approaches conservation through a combination of technical environmental engineering expertise and community-based strategies. His work emphasizes that true sustainability requires balancing ecological needs with human wellbeing, creating solutions that benefit both wildlife and local communities. This holistic approach has been central to his success in implementing lasting conservation programs across Indonesia's diverse ecosystems.

Through his leadership at Prakriti Nusantara, Bumi continues to pioneer innovative conservation models that integrate traditional ecological knowledge with modern scientific approaches, creating scalable solutions for Indonesia's most pressing environmental challenges.
